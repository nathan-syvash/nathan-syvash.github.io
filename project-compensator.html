Controls Project

PD Controller for Autonomous Vehicle

Modeled, designed, and validated a PD-controlled autonomous car that used an IR sensor to detect and avoid obstacles while maintaining a stable set distance from a wall.

Focus
System identification, sensor characterization, MATLAB sisotool, analog compensator design, experimental validation
System
DC motor-driven duck car with IR distance sensing and analog PD compensator
Outcome
Reduced overshoot from 18% to below 5%, cut settling time to 1.8 s, and achieved accurate wall tracking with no oscillations.

Overview

This project involved developing and validating a PD control system for a DC motor-driven autonomous car tasked with proximity maintenance. Using an IR sensor, the car maintained a set distance from a wall and reacted to sudden disturbances with fast, stable responses. Driving system and the IR sensor were characterized experimentally and linearized around the operating point.

My work combined system identification, compensator design, and experimental validation to achieve precise, real-world performance improvements.

My Role

  1. System Identification. Modeled the electromechanical plant by experimentally determining motor gain and time constant from velocity versus voltage trials.
  2. Sensor Characterization. Linearized IR sensor output around the 10 cm operating point and derived a gain of –8.94 V/m.
  3. Controller Design. Used MATLAB sisotool to tune PD compensator parameters, including pole, zero, and gain, while ensuring at least 30° phase margin and infinite gain margin.
  4. Implementation. Built an analog compensator circuit using op-amps and discrete R/C components, then tuned resistor and capacitor values to match theoretical pole-zero placement.
  5. Testing. Validated with step-input experiments, logged transient response, and tuned gain to reduce overshoot and improve settling.

The final design exceeded expectations: the car responded quickly and precisely, with no overshoot or oscillations, and came within 1 cm (0.4 in) of the desired operating point.

Validation

Experimental Results

  • Uncompensated Motor. Underdamped response with 18% overshoot and about 4.5 s settling time.
  • PD Compensator. Reduced overshoot to below 5%, cut settling time to 1.8 s, and achieved tight tracking to within 0.5 in of the setpoint.
  • Robustness. Recharacterized the plant and retuned the compensator after hardware replacement, including a new IR sensor package, while preserving closed-loop performance.

MATLAB Analysis

  • Root Locus & Bode. Verified no right-half-plane poles and achieved at least 72.6° phase margin.
  • Transient Analysis. Confirmed rise-time improvement and oscillation suppression with derivative action.

Challenges Overcome

  1. Sensor Failure. After blowing a fuse and frying an IR sensor, recharacterized the plant with a conservative time constant of τ = 0.6 s and tuned the compensator accordingly.
  2. Hardware Variability. Managed component tolerances, where final resistor and capacitor values deviated from nominal and required recalculation of effective pole-zero placement.
  3. Noise Sensitivity. Balanced derivative action to reduce oscillations without amplifying sensor noise.

Outcomes

  • Delivered a validated PD controller that met target specifications for stability, speed, and robustness.
  • Documented a reproducible workflow for plant modeling, compensator tuning, and experimental validation in MATLAB, Simulink, and hardware implementation.
  • Demonstrated the ability to bridge theoretical control design with hands-on circuit building and testing.
  • Achieved fast, accurate, and stable distance regulation relevant to real-world autonomous and robotic control problems.
